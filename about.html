About Empathy Spectrum Disorder

A framework for understanding empathy as a dynamic capacity that can become intensified, constrained, fragmented, or strategically modulated across development and lived experience.

What is ESD?

Empathy Spectrum Disorder (ESD) is a proposed diagnostic framework for identifying patterns of empathy dysregulation that traditional diagnoses often fail to capture. It addresses a wide range of presentations, including emotional blunting, empathic flooding, cognitive-only empathy, and strategic (dark) empathy.

Why it matters

Existing models such as ASD, NPD, or CPTSD can provide helpful information, but they often offer limited language for understanding empathy as a flexible, adaptive capacity. ESD reframes empathy as a biological and relational process influenced by nervous system regulation, trauma history, neurodiversity, and environmental feedback.

How ESD is used

The goal of ESD is practical clarity. The framework is designed to help clinicians, educators, and caregivers name what is happening, map patterns of dysregulation, and choose interventions that fit the individual rather than forcing empathy into a single “high vs low” interpretation.

Meet the creator

Ayesha Qahash, M.A.

ESD was developed by Ayesha Qahash, M.A., whose academic background in criminology, psychology, and individualized studies at Goddard College led to the creation of the model. Her work integrates neuroscience, trauma theory, and emotional development into a new language for understanding empathy in the 21st century.
